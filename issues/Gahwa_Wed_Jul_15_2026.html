The Lead
​
While the world watched Hormuz, the Houthis brought the war back to Saudi soil.
Saudi air defenses intercepted Houthi missiles and drones aimed at Abha International Airport on Tuesday, the first direct attack on the Kingdom’s territory since the UN-brokered truce collapsed, according to coalition officials. In the same 24-hour window, Iranian cruise missiles struck two UAE-flagged oil tankers, the Mombasa and Al Bahiyah, killing one crew member and wounding eight, Emirati officials said. It was the deadliest day for commercial shipping in the Hormuz corridor since the US-Iran ceasefire collapsed.

Washington reimposed its naval blockade on Iranian oil exports and then abruptly scrapped the proposed 20 percent cargo toll, pivoting to Gulf trade and investment deals. The Gulf’s economic decoupling from Hormuz shifted from PowerPoint to concrete this week.
Operators must immediately reassess maritime risk, insurance costs, and alternative supply routes; the Hormuz bypass is no longer a contingency, it is the base case.

GCC & SAUDI
SAR 699-a-Month Home Loans
First-time Saudi homebuyers in Riyadh, Jeddah, and Dammam can now secure a mortgage for just SAR 699 a month under the Real Estate Development Fund’s newly launched Alternative Financing Program. Saudi National Bank is the first banking partner, offering instant approval, with more lenders expected to follow.

The launch targets the stubborn gap to the 70 percent homeownership Vision 2030 goal; Knight Frank estimates an affordability ceiling around SAR 1.5 million per home. At SAR 699, the payment works out cheaper than many monthly car leases, a deliberate push to convert renters into owners.

The program arrives just as new residential mortgages in the Kingdom fell by more than half over the past year and PIF-mandated austerity places megaprojects under review. Low-cost state-backed debt for ordinary Saudis is one of the strongest demand-side levers the housing ministry has pulled yet. Banks, developers, and real estate agents should prepare for a surge in first-time buyer applications; the SAR 699 price floor resets the affordability conversation overnight.

GCC & SAUDI
Neom Quietly Kills The Line
Neom has quietly erased its most famous promises from the official website. References to housing nine million people, banning roads and cars, and running on 100 percent renewable energy are gone. The Line, once a 170-kilometer, 500-meter-tall mirror city, is now described as ‘a core component’ of a ‘phased, demand-led approach’, an initial 2.4 km stretch for roughly 300,000 residents.

The luxury island Sindalah lost its standalone section; Magna’s page erased 15 planned boutique hotels including the Epicon skyscraper. The retreat tracks PIF’s wider belt-tightening: project budgets cut by as much as 60 percent, capex expected to fall 15 percent this year, and around $16 billion in contracts facing cancellation, including a $5 billion Trojena package tied to the now-lost Asian Winter Games.

Executives call it a routine website update, but the edits align with a hard pivot toward Expo 2030 and the 2034 World Cup. The $8.4 billion green hydrogen joint venture with Acwa and Air Products is advancing, and Neom’s data center ambitions slot into PIF’s national AI strategy. The sci-fi renders are fading; the infrastructure bets remain. Contractors, consultants, and investors betting on Neom’s original masterplan need to re-price assumptions, the project is shifting from a singular megacity to a portfolio of phased, event-driven investments.

AI & TECH
88% of UAE Firms AI-Locked
A sweeping IBM survey of UAE business leaders exposes a dangerous dependency: 88 percent say switching their primary AI vendor would be difficult, and 96 percent admit they don’t fully grasp their organisation’s AI dependencies across models, vendors, and infrastructure.

UAE firms averaged seven AI-related disruptions over the past two years. 84 percent said a seven-day vendor outage would cause severe or critical disruption, and 78 percent would accept a 20 percent cost increase to keep their current AI vendors if it improved strategic flexibility. Only 7 percent of enterprises operate at the most advanced AI control level, those that do protect 55 percent more operating profit.

With the region racing to embed AI while geopolitical tension severs supply chains, the numbers suggest the UAE’s AI equation must shift from adoption speed toward sovereign capability and vendor diversity. Vendor lock-in is no longer a vendor’s problem; it’s a balance-sheet risk. CIOs and procurement heads should immediately map AI dependency chains across models, cloud providers, and APIs; a seven-day outage scenario is now a mandatory board-level stress test.

CULTURE
Saudi Women Storm Esports
Women now make up 20 percent of Saudi Arabia’s 2,000-plus esports players, a share that towers over the five percent global female participation rate. The stat, shared during the Esports World Cup in Paris, points to a deliberate talent pipeline built under Vision 2030’s gaming and entertainment strategy.

UAE club Nigma Galaxy stormed through the Dota 2 group stage, winning nine of ten maps to reach the quarter-finals. Three more wins would deliver the first EWC trophy to an Emirati club. The tournament itself drew more than 2,000 players from 200 clubs across 100 countries, competing for over $75 million in prize money.

For a region investing billions in smart cities and entertainment tourism, esports is a soft-power asset with a built-in youth demographic. Saudi Arabia’s ability to bring women into the arena while the rest of the world lags offers a measurable edge, and a narrative the Kingdom’s critics struggle to answer. Brands and agencies targeting Gen Z in the Gulf should shift esports spend toward women’s teams and mixed formats; the 20 percent figure is a marketing narrative that global peers cannot yet replicate.

GCC & SAUDI
DP World Plots Fujairah Port
DP World is in talks to build a new multipurpose port and container terminal in Fujairah, with an initial investment in the hundreds of millions of dollars and a possible construction timeline of just 18 months. The project would add a deepwater hub on the UAE’s Indian Ocean coast, entirely outside the Strait of Hormuz.

The urgency is written in the numbers. A dedicated terminal on the ocean side would unlock capacity that currently sits inside a military chokepoint.

Moody’s expects DP World’s earnings to slip to $5.9 billion this year from $6.6 billion, but a Fujairah expansion signals the ports giant is betting that geography, in the long run, beats geopolitics. Logistics operators and cargo owners should evaluate Fujairah as a permanent new node in regional supply chains, not a war-time stopgap.
