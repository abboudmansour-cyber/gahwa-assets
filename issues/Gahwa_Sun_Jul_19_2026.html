The Lead
​
Iran’s missiles hit a Kuwaiti desalination plant and oil facility, officials say.
Iran’s missiles struck a Kuwaiti desalination plant and an oil facility, according to officials, marking the first direct hit on Gulf infrastructure since the current round of fighting began. The attacks reportedly came on the seventh straight night of U.S. airstrikes against Iranian military targets. The IRGC claimed it had also mined two oil tankers and stopped four vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, a claim CENTCOM quickly denied. Still, Hormuz tanker crossings fell to a three-week low, and international oil firms started halting production in Iraq’s Kurdistan region.

For Gulf operators, the moment shifts the calculus from watching a distant war to managing a direct physical threat. Every port operator, insurer, and logistics planner now has to price in a widening conflict zone that may not respect any border.

The conflict is also accelerating a parallel infrastructure race. Iraq and Syria signed an agreement this week to rebuild an oil pipeline that would bypass Hormuz entirely, a project suddenly less theoretical than a month ago. When wars reshape pipelines, they reshape decades of trade geography.

The Rundown

MACRO & GEO
Iraq, Syria Bypass Hormuz With Pipeline
Iraq and Syria signed an agreement to rebuild an oil pipeline that would let crude flow straight to the Mediterranean, bypassing the Strait of Hormuz entirely. The deal lands on a day when Hormuz crossings hit a three-week low and tanker insurance costs are spiraling.

The old Kirkuk, Banias pipeline has been shut for over a decade, a victim of war and neglect. Reviving it would give Baghdad and Damascus a strategic lever, and give global oil markets a route that does not rely on a waterway dominated by Iranian gunboats. For Gulf producers, the pipeline is both a relief valve and a quiet warning that their chokepoint advantage can be circumvented.

Even if the project takes years, the signal today matters. Infrastructure that was once geopolitically impossible is suddenly getting signatures. Supply-chain planners in the Gulf should watch how quickly the financing and engineering talks follow the politics. Iraq and Syria signed the deal as Iran's threats to Hormuz turn an old pipeline idea into an urgent strategic alternative.

MACRO & GEO
Kurdistan Oil Output Halts on Conflict
International oil firms suspended production in Iraq’s Kurdistan region, citing security risks from the escalating U.S.-Iran conflict. Security costs and personnel safety are now impossible to guarantee near the Iraqi-Iranian border zone. Gulf buyers who rely on blended Kurdish grades will need to pivot, likely bidding up alternatives from Saudi Arabia and the UAE in the short run.

For regional operators, it is a preview of how quickly an oil war in one corner of the neighborhood fractures supply chains elsewhere. The halt follows the deaths of two U.S. service members in Jordan and the seventh night of American airstrikes on Iran.

AI & TECH
Kimi K3 Rivals Top AI Models
Chinese lab Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, an open-weight model with a claimed 2.8 trillion parameters that matches GPT-5.6 and Claude Fable 5 on key benchmarks, but costs half as much. At $15 per million output tokens, it undercuts the frontier by a margin that developers will notice immediately.

The release cements a pattern: Chinese models from Tencent, Xiaomi, DeepSeek and MiniMax now occupy the top five slots on OpenRouter by weekly token usage. The performance gap between East and West in language AI is essentially gone, even if U.S. labs still lead on the most dangerous cyber capabilities.

For Gulf tech investors and government AI programmes, the message is practical. Open-weight models from China are now a viable alternative to the U.S. frontier duopoly, particularly for Arabic-language fine-tuning where cost sensitivity runs high. Every national cloud strategy written six months ago assumed a two-vendor world; that assumption is now out of date. Kimi K3 became the strongest open-weight model available this week, intensifying the open-source AI race as U.S. chip controls tighten.

CULTURE
Cut Your DEWA Bill With AC Hacks
Simple tweaks to your air conditioning can trim Dubai electricity bills during peak summer, as temperatures push AC units to run nonstop. Setting the thermostat to 24°C instead of 19°C, cleaning filters, and sealing window gaps are among the lowest-effort moves with the highest monthly savings.

The advice lands as DEWA summer tariffs bite and households see bills climb past AED 1,500 for a villa. Smart thermostat adoption is rising in Dubai villa communities, but the cheapest fix is still behavior: running the AC at a steady moderate temperature rather than treating it like an on-off switch every time someone enters a room.

It is a low-stakes, high-return habit shift for anyone paying a utility bill in the Gulf right now. The same principles apply to office cooling, making it a conversation worth raising with facilities managers as well. With August approaching and ACs running 24/7, a handful of adjustments could cut hundreds of dirhams off the next DEWA statement.

Alserkal Avenue’s New Café Gems
Alserkal Avenue is now home to a growing cluster of bakeries and plant-based cafés, turning the arts district into a weekend destination for more than gallery hopping. New openings include a sourdough-focused bakery and a vegan café that doubles as a co-working spot, both leaning into the warehouse-chic aesthetic the Avenue is known for.

The shift mirrors a broader Dubai trend: specialty coffee and brunch culture are moving from Jumeirah and DIFC into grittier, creative neighborhoods. Footfall data from the Avenue’s management shows weekend traffic up noticeably since the start of the year, driven in part by food-and-beverage rather than pure art visits.

For F&B operators, it confirms that a location play in an established cultural quarter can pull in digital-native crowds without paying mall rents. Expect the lane between Al Quoz and the beach to get increasingly competitive. Summer weekends are driving café-hopping indoors; the Avenue’s new spots give a reason to explore beyond the usual Marina and Downtown circuits.
