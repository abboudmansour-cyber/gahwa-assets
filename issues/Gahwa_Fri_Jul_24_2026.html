The Lead
​
​
Riyadh locked in a 30-year nuclear deal that lets it enrich uranium at home, now Congress gets 90 days to find a reason to say no.
The 30-year agreement lets Saudi Arabia enrich uranium on its own soil, turning a decade of diplomatic haggling into a signed bilateral pact. Energy Secretary Chris Wright and Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman inked the 123 Agreement alongside a safeguards deal, conspicuously omitting the 'gold standard' that would have barred enrichment and spent‑fuel reprocessing. A joint feasibility study on a domestic enrichment facility now begins, and US reactor builders, Westinghouse, Bechtel, BWXT, Centrus, can finally chase the kingdom’s long‑stalled tender for two reactors at Khor Duwaiheen, roughly 2.9 GWe.

The paperwork lands on a Hill that has already grumbled: the agreement must survive 90 continuous session days of congressional review. Opponents are making noise, but the smart money says they lack the votes to derail a deal the White House wants. If it stands, Riyadh can move toward its target of ~17 GW of nuclear capacity by 2040, covering about 15% of projected electricity demand without burning a single barrel of exportable crude.

For the Gulf, the macro take is blunt: a Saudi‑US nuclear supply chain is now real. Regional utilities watching the UAE’s Barakah model just saw Washington green‑light a far more ambitious cycle. Every downstream industrial plan, hydrogen, steel, data centers, just got a new baseload option. Congress gets its say, but the architecture of a Saudi nuclear industry was drawn up today.
The signed agreement now enters its 90-day Congressional review window. Nuclear equipment suppliers and regional energy planners should model a Saudi reactor program that is no longer hypothetical.

MACRO & GEO
Houthis Reportedly Strike Saudi Tankers, Oil Hits $100
Houthi militants reportedly struck two Saudi-flagged oil tankers, the Encelia and Layla, in the Red Sea. The Encelia was left ablaze at the bow; SPA confirmed all crew are safe. Five tankers changed course in the Red Sea on Wednesday, two rerouting toward the Suez Canal, and tanker traffic is now nearly nonexistent. Brent crude reportedly jumped past $95 a barrel immediately after the attack and continued climbing, touching $101.93 before settling above the $100 mark. The US reportedly carried out a 12th consecutive night of strikes against Iran as Kuwait’s air defences intercepted hostile drones and missiles aimed at the Al‑Abdali border post. CENTCOM has reportedly redirected 12 commercial vessels and disabled one ship since the naval blockade against Iran resumed. For Gulf economies, the insurance premium on any energy logistics that touches these waters has just repriced sharply, and every barrel leaving the region must now factor in a risk premium that shows no sign of fading. The attack on Saudi‑owned vessels is reportedly the first confirmed strike on Gulf tankers since the Iran‑US ceasefire collapsed, and it forces every regional crude exporter to reassess Red Sea routing immediately.

GCC & SAUDI
DP World Builds Hormuz Bypass
DP World inked an in‑principle agreement with Fujairah Ports Authority to develop two deepwater terminals on the Gulf of Oman under a 50‑year concession. The Al Rugaylat terminal will handle up to 2.5 million TEUs, 1.7 million tons of general cargo and 190,000 car‑equivalent units a year; the Dibba terminal adds another 3.6 million tons of general cargo capacity. Combined, they lift DP World’s UAE container handling by roughly 13% to nearly 22 million TEUs.

Both sites sit outside the Strait of Hormuz and link to Jebel Ali and Jafza through the group’s inland logistics network. The move mirrors the UAE’s broader east‑coast push: Adnoc is fast‑tracking a second pipeline to Fujairah, and Borouge and AD Ports are exploring a petrochemical export hub on the same coast. Construction is phased and expected to take 24, 30 months once ground is broken.

The strategic logic is as clear as the Fujairah skyline: a logistics corridor that does not depend on Hormuz is no longer an ambition, it is an asset on the balance sheet. Every shipper who watches naval blockades in the Strait will now price in a credible Emirati alternative. The in‑principle deal was announced today, and engineering and construction phasing details give supply chain operators a clear timeline to factor into their risk models.

AI & TECH
PIF’s $55B EA Takeover Clears EU
PIF’s near‑$1 trillion asset base makes the check size manageable, and EA’s franchises give the kingdom a ready‑made global entertainment pipeline.

For the Gulf, the EA deal is a reminder that Saudi capital can muscle its way into the top tier of an industry where live‑service revenue models are far stickier than oil export cycles. Once Brussels signs off on the subsidies review, the transaction moves from ‘mega’ to ‘done’ almost overnight. The EU Phase I approval landed today; the Foreign Subsidies Regulation decision is due by July 30, so the deal’s final shape will be clear within a week.

FINTECH & CRYPTO
Kuwait’s $6B Bond Three Times Oversubscribed
Kuwait raised $6 billion from a three‑tranche sovereign bond that drew over $18 billion in orders, a three‑times oversubscription that signals investor appetite for Gulf paper remains strong even as oil prices spike. The tranches: $3 billion in 3‑year notes at 70 bps over US Treasuries, $1.5 billion in 5‑year at 75 bps, and $1.5 billion in 10‑year at 85 bps.

Allocation skewed heavily toward the Americas (48%) and UK/Europe (28%), with MENA investors taking 16%. Finance Minister Yaqoub Al‑Refaei cited confidence in the country’s credit position. The issuance follows a March 2025 law setting a KD30 billion ($97.4 billion) public debt ceiling, and Kuwait has now borrowed over $11 billion in the past year.

For Gulf debt markets, the order book is a real‑time vote of confidence at a moment when regional security fears might have priced out some buyers. Kuwait’s deficit trajectory remains steep, the 2026‑27 budget projects a KD9.8 billion shortfall, but the market is clearly treating the debt ceiling as an opportunity, not a ceiling. The final pricing and oversubscription figures were released today, giving treasurers and sovereign wealth funds fresh benchmarks for GCC credit in a high‑oil‑price environment.

GCC & SAUDI
Aldar Drops AED 100B on Saadiyat Island
Aldar launched Marsa Al Saadiyat, a AED 100 billion waterfront district that will house more than 58,000 residents on the final undeveloped slice of Saadiyat Island. The plan includes an 8 km shoreline with 5.6 km of beaches, a marina with over 350 berths, two luxury hotels, and a 6,000‑capacity theatre district.

The project is 50% larger than London’s Canary Wharf and already has a price anchor: a luxury eight‑bedroom beachfront mansion sold for £81 million in 2025, setting an Abu Dhabi record. A scenic walkway will link the district directly to the Saadiyat Cultural District, home to the Louvre, Zayed National Museum and the upcoming Guggenheim, creating a continuous luxury corridor from museum door to marina slip.

For the UAE property market, Marsa Al Saadiyat is the clearest signal yet that Abu Dhabi intends to compete with Dubai’s waterfront trophy projects on scale, not charm. When the first homes hit the market in the second half of 2026, the capital’s real‑estate pitch will have shifted from ‘cultural oasis’ to ‘fully alarmed coastal statement.’ The project was formally launched today, with site works beginning later in 2026 and first sales scheduled for the second half of the year.
