The Lead
​
One of the world’s two main oil arteries is under direct fire, and the other is being held hostage.
Brent crude futures jumped 70 cents to $84.93 a barrel, capping a 9% weekly rise.

The simultaneous threats to the Strait of Hormuz and the Bab al-Mandab are forcing tanker operators and oil majors to reassess every barrel. Capital Economics warns that a closure of Bab al-Mandab could trigger a global recession. Iran has reportedly asked Houthi allies to prepare to shut the Red Sea export route, while Oxford Economics calculates a $1/barrel toll on Hormuz transits could net Iran and Oman $6.8 billion annually, a proposal the UAE’s Adnoc chief brands ‘economic terrorism’.

For Gulf states, this is no longer a distant proxy fight. Direct hits on bases in Bahrain and Kuwait put the entire GCC at the centre of a widening war. The region’s sovereign risk premium has gapped out to 402 basis points over Treasuries, the widest in almost four years. With oil demand forecast to decline this year, producers are doubly exposed: to security shocks that spike prices and to demand destruction if the conflict spirals.

The US has shifted from a naval blockade to pursuing an alternative Iraq, Syria pipeline, signalling a long-term recalibration of energy corridors. That leaves Gulf exporters facing a future where their most valuable asset, unfettered access to the world’s oil markets, is being redrawn under fire.
Reassess supply-chain risk and energy exposure as the war moves directly onto GCC soil for the first time.

e& Sells Vodafone Stake, Retreats to Core
UAE telecoms giant e& has sold its full 16% stake in Vodafone for $5.95 billion, generating a net return of roughly $1.3 billion and signalling a sharp U-turn under new CEO Masood M. Sharif Mahmood. The sale, to French billionaire Xavier Niel, who becomes Vodafone’s largest shareholder, is the centrepiece of a wider retreat from global expansion. HSBC expects the disposal to cut e&’s net debt-to-EBITDA to about 0.5x by end-2026.

The unwinding extends beyond the Vodafone exit. e& is reviewing its venture capital arm, e& capital, and SME lender Beehive as it refocuses on core telecom operations. The company had spent years building a portfolio of tech and digital assets across the Middle East, Africa, and Europe; now it is shedding them to strengthen a balance sheet that had been stretched by acquisition debt.

For the UAE’s corporate champions, the move is a cautionary tale: even the best-capitalised state-backed firms are reassessing risk after a year of regional turmoil and higher borrowing costs. The sale returns e& to its roots just as the domestic market faces new competition and a government push to standardise higher education licensing, a reminder that local consolidation, not foreign adventure, is the order of the day. Re-evaluate exposure to UAE conglomerates unwinding global bets; focus shifts to domestic consolidation plays.

GCC & SAUDI
Dubai Home Prices Down 10% Since February
Dubai’s housing market delivered a record 27,300 residential units in the second quarter, but the flood of supply landed in a market where buyer appetite is cooling fast. Transactions fell 19% quarter-on-quarter to 35,900, and ValuStrat’s June index showed residential values slipping another 1% month-on-month, taking the cumulative decline since late February to 10%. Apartment prices dropped 4% in three months; even villas, long the hot segment, edged down 0.8%.

Rents are following suit. Annual Ejari registrations fell 22% quarter-on-quarter, and major communities saw rents drop 8, 10%. New residence permits declined roughly 15%, a sign that the population growth that fuelled the boom is decelerating. Savills now expects population growth to moderate to around 2% this year, down sharply from 4.1% in 2022.

Developers have slammed the brakes on launches: only about 5,300 units entered planning in Q2, compared with more than 45,000 in the first quarter. Emaar is pressing ahead with its AED 200 billion Dubai Mansions mega-project, but the wider market is shifting from a seller’s race to a refinancing drive, 70% of valuation activity by quarter-end was for refinancing, historically double the norm. For operators, the correction means pricing power is shifting back to tenants and end-users for the first time in years. Rethink pricing and inventory strategies now; the market has flipped from landlord to tenant leverage.

AI & TECH
Saudi AI Firm Pledges 50 MW to Cohere
Saudi technology company Humaine has signed a deal to allocate at least 50 megawatts of AI compute capacity to Canadian AI lab Cohere, with the potential to scale further over five years. The agreement, reported by Zero to One, positions the Kingdom as a physical host for the infrastructure that underpins large language models, a shift from simply importing AI to running the servers that power it.

Cohere, one of the best-funded independent AI firms, gets a home for its models in a jurisdiction with generous energy subsidies and a government that has made AI infrastructure a pillar of Vision 2030. For Humaine, the tie-up puts it on the map as a domestic compute provider at a time when global demand for AI inference is growing faster than data centre supply.

The deal mirrors Groq’s $1.5 billion Saudi commitment announced at LEAP 2025, and it comes as regional sovereign funds are scouring the world for AI assets. If the 50 MW allocation grows, Saudi Arabia could become a Middle East hub for training and serving models for the enterprise market across the GCC. Map where your AI compute will live; Saudi is rapidly building capacity and want long-term anchor tenants.

CULTURE
Indonesian Restaurant Opens 100m Above Dubai
Andaliman, a new Indonesian fine-dining spot, has opened at The Link in One Za’abeel, perched 100 metres above the Dubai skyline with one of the city’s most dramatic views. The restaurant introduces a cuisine rarely seen in the emirate, betting that Dubai’s luxury diners are ready for something beyond the usual pan-Asian and Levantine offerings.

The opening lands at a time when the F&B sector is still absorbing the slowdown in new residency permits and a dip in high-net-worth arrivals. But distinctive concepts in trophy locations, One Za’abeel is already a draw, continue to pull in footfall. If Andaliman can win over a critical mass of adventurous eaters and the influencer set, it could signal a broader appetite for niche cuisines in Dubai’s competitive dining scene. Try the tasting menu before the influencer rush; a rare cuisine entry in a landmark address is worth a booking.

GCC & SAUDI
Uber’s $14.8B Deal Hands It Talabat
Uber launched a $14.8 billion takeover bid for Delivery Hero, offering €41.50 per share and securing an indirect grip on DFM-listed Talabat, the Middle East’s dominant food delivery platform. Uber already owns 24.8% of Delivery Hero; with Prosus committing to tender, its total economic interest reaches roughly 53%. Talabat confirmed its operations, strategy, leadership, and listing on the Dubai Financial Market will stay unchanged.

Talabat reported gross merchandise value of $2.7 billion in Q1, up 19% year-on-year, with net income of $86 million and raised FY 2026 guidance to $300, 330 million. The acquisition, expected to close in the second half of 2027, turns Uber into a delivery juggernaut across the GCC, where Talabat holds a commanding lead.

For Dubai’s capital markets, the deal is a test: a $10 billion homegrown tech champion will sit inside a US-listed giant, but keep its local listing. Investors will watch whether that structure protects the free float or slowly starves it of liquidity. Watch Talabat’s free float: a dual-listed structure inside a US giant could shift liquidity, so position ahead of closure.
