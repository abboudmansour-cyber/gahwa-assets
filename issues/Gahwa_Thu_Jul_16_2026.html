The Lead
​
Saudi Arabia orders ministries to slash non-essential spending by half as the budget deficit balloons past $74 billion, putting a chill through the gig economy that rides on government contracts.
Saudi government entities have been told to cut non-essential spending by up to 50%, freeze all new hiring, and axe education allowances for employees' children from next year. Travel per diems are slashed and non-essential business trips are effectively grounded. The belt-tightening, circulated early this week, also pauses recruitment at several entities until further notice.

The order lands after a year in which the budget deficit swelled to $74bn, more than double the original projection, as lower and more volatile oil prices squeezed revenues. Spending controls were already in motion before the current Iran crisis, but the war has added urgency.

For any operator living off government contracts, consultancies, event agencies, IT vendors, the instruction is unambiguous: the era of blank cheques is winding down. The PIF, meanwhile, is signing non-binding memorandums rather than hard commitments, reinforcing the sense that Riyadh is now scrutinising every riyal. Vision 2030's breakneck dreams are meeting the finance ministry's red pen.
Circulated this week as government entities received formal directives, making immediate cost-control measures the new baseline for every supplier.
The Rundown
​
​
MACRO & GEO
Gulf Grinds to a Halt as Iran Strikes Widen
Houthi rebels also fired a missile at Abha airport, according to reports, marking the first such strike on the Saudi city in four years. The exchanges sent Brent crude above $86 a barrel.

European air-safety regulators warned airlines to avoid the airspace of Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar and the UAE; British Airways scrapped all services to the Emirates until October. For the Gulf, the war is no longer a distant headline, it's hitting stock exchanges (ADX and DFM slid every session this week), raising shipping insurance, and forcing Bahrain to accuse Iran of blackmail through maritime threats. With Trump swapping his Hormuz toll for vague investment pledges, the region is left to absorb the economic blowback largely on its own. Strikes, blockade, and airspace warnings all intensified within the last 48 hours, redrawing risk maps for every business operating across the Gulf.
​
GCC & SAUDI
China Cuts Saudi Crude Orders to Zero
Chinese refiners have requested zero Saudi crude cargoes for August, slashing the Kingdom's shipments to a trickle as China's total oil imports plunged 41% year-on-year to a near-decade low. Saudi allocations had already halved to 20 million barrels in May from 40 million in April, with cheaper Russian and Iranian barrels continuing to elbow out the Kingdom's core customer.

The import slump is a demand-side alarm: China's economy expanded just 4.3% in Q2, its slowest in three years, and its refineries are swimming in cut-price alternatives. For Saudi Aramco, this translates into a long-term market-share battle that no amount of Vision 2030 diversification can offset quickly. August loading programmes are being finalised now, locking in a sharp drop that forces Saudi Arabia to hunt for other buyers or cut output.
​
AI & TECH
91% of Saudi Firms Say AI Works
A sweeping survey by SAP and YouGov found 91% of Saudi organisations say their artificial intelligence projects are meeting or exceeding expectations, but only 59% have tied those initiatives to a company-wide strategy. The enthusiasm matches the government's Vision 2030 push, yet the gap between pilot success and enterprise-scale governance remains wide.

For tech vendors and system integrators, the message is that Saudi clients have appetite and early wins, but the real money will flow to those who can stitch isolated models into an auditable, compliant architecture, especially as regulators start circling AI decisions in banking and government. The data gives operators a clear signal to shift pitches from 'try AI' to 'govern AI at scale' as the next Saudi budget cycle approaches.
​
CULTURE
Comic Con Returns to Abu Dhabi
Middle East Film & Comic Con sets up camp at Abu Dhabi's ADNEC Centre this September for three days of cosplay, celebrity guests, and the kind of pop-culture mania the capital usually reserves for F1 weekend. After a sleepier summer, the return of a dedicated geek gathering signals the emirate's appetite for experiential events that pull in younger crowds and regional merchandise spend.

For mall operators and HORECA, it's a blueprint: footfall doesn't just come from luxury openings; sometimes it walks in dressed as a Stormtrooper. Tickets are about to go on sale, giving tourism and retail teams a precise event to plan campaigns around before the autumn season begins.
​
GCC & SAUDI
Lucid Slumps, Then Denies Bankruptcy
Lucid Group rushed to deny a media report that it was weighing bankruptcy or a go-private deal, calling the speculation 'categorically false,' after its shares tanked 16% and triggered a volatility halt. The EV maker, majority-owned by Saudi Arabia's PIF, said it has enough liquidity into next year and pointed to a fresh $550mn injection from PIF's Ayar vehicle and $200mn from Uber.

Still, the panic underscores the market's jitters: Lucid is burning cash, its new CEO Silvio Napoli is overseeing a restructuring with AlixPartner, and the company's fate remains inextricably tied to the Kingdom's willingness to keep writing cheques, a willingness now under its own spending review. The stock collapse and denial happened in a single trading session, forcing PIF-backed entities to reassure markets immediately.
