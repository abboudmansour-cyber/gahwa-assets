The Lead
​
​
Iran hits Bahrain and Kuwait; Houthis shut the Red Sea.
Iran struck US-allied Bahrain and Kuwait with drones and missiles, officials said, widening a conflict that has already killed 17 American troops. The strikes hit military infrastructure in Riffa and Muharraq, the IRGC claimed, while a separate attack reportedly targeted a US base in Kuwait. The escalation came as the US completed its tenth consecutive night of air raids on Iranian targets, and the Houthis, Iran’s Yemeni ally, announced they would close the Red Sea to all vessels bound for Saudi ports.

More than 7 million barrels of crude passed through the Bab al‑Mandab choke point daily in June, and the blockade threatens to sever a crucial Saudi export route. Brent crude jumped 4.3% to $88.62, its highest in weeks, as traders priced in a prolonged supply disruption. Ceasefire efforts are faltering: mediators floated a 10‑day pause, but Iran reportedly insists it must retain control of the Strait of Hormuz. Already, Reuters polling shows Kuwait and Qatar’s economies are on track to shrink 8.1% this year, the UAE 0.5%, while Oman edges up 3.1% and Saudi Arabia, whose east‑west pipeline bypasses the strait, manages 1.4% growth. Businesses are pricing in a permanent geopolitical risk premium, economists warn, which will bleed into higher borrowing costs and lower investment even if guns fall silent.
If you trade or ship through the region, assume both Hormuz and Bab al‑Mandab are risk corridors for the foreseeable future.
The Rundown

GCC & SAUDI
SVC backs SEEDRA Fund II to fuel Saudi tech startups
Saudi Venture Capital (SVC) has committed to SEEDRA Ventures Fund II, doubling down on the kingdom’s early‑stage tech pipeline. The fund, which was oversubscribed, targets startups in AI, fintech, proptech, SaaS, and logistics, sectors central to Vision 2030’s diversification push. SVC, a subsidiary of the SME Bank and part of the National Development Fund, has been deploying capital since 2018, but this marks a continued bet on home‑grown venture at a time when regional instability might otherwise freeze risk capital.

Haitham Alforaih is managing partner of SEEDRA Ventures, while Nora Alsarhan leads SVC. The move signals that state‑linked capital still sees a strong domestic startup pipeline, even as overall MENA investment banking fees fell 19% in the first half. For founders, it means a major LP is still writing cheques. If you are raising a seed round in the kingdom, this new dry powder is real and heading to early‑stage deals.

GCC & SAUDI
Riyadh Air orders 34 more jets, opens Madrid route
Riyadh Air exercised purchase rights for 34 widebody aircraft, adding 28 Boeing 787s and six Airbus A350s to a fleet that only recently took its first delivery. The orders, unveiled at the Farnborough Airshow, bring the Saudi startup’s confirmed fleet to 91 aircraft, with 19 options remaining. CEO Tony Douglas also launched the airline’s first flights to Madrid, signing an MoU with Air Europa for codesharing, loyalty reciprocity, and cargo cooperation.

The airline, which aims to generate over 200,000 jobs and contribute $20 billion to non‑oil GDP by 2030, now sells tickets to 12 cities and has US regulatory approval to fly. The rapid build‑up, six planes arrived in a single month, tests operational mettle, but it underscores the kingdom’s determination to create a global hub even as regional conflict disrupts Gulf airspace. Etihad and Emirates have canceled flights, giving Riyadh Air a chance to grab market share when the skies reopen. Route planners and aviation suppliers should watch Riyadh Air’s execution speed; it is building a hub while competitors are grounded.

AI & TECH
Think raises $8M for Saudi AI infrastructure
Riyadh‑based startup Think has closed a pre‑seed round of over $8 million, co‑led by Raed Ventures and Wa’ed Ventures, to scale manufacturing of AI hardware. The company builds AI infrastructure, servers, storage, and networking gear optimized for machine learning workloads, aiming to supply local data centers and enterprises. The raise is one of the first pre‑seed rounds in the Gulf explicitly targeting AI hardware rather than software, a bet that Saudi’s growing cloud and compute demand will need home‑grown kit.

The investment comes as TSMC commits another $100 billion to Arizona, underscoring the global scramble for AI capacity. For Gulf startups, Think’s round signals that deep‑tech hardware can attract early‑stage capital, even in a region where software and fintech have dominated. Founders building compute‑intensive AI products in the Gulf now have a local hardware play to watch and potentially partner with.

CULTURE
Lay's Debuts Saudi Pickled Lemon Chip
Lay’s has launched a limited‑edition Saudi Pickled Lemon flavour, using 100% locally grown potatoes and starring national‑team heroes in its campaign. The FIFA World Cup sponsor released the flavour alongside global ones, Brazilian BBQ & Honey, Mexican Pineapple Jalapeño, French Sour Cream & Herbs, but the Saudi variant is the only one made entirely with domestic spuds. The ads feature Salem Al Dawsari, Musab AlJuwayr, and legends Mohammed Noor and Hussein Abdulghani.

The campaign is a crisp example of how global brands are tailoring World Cup hype to local identity: locally sourced, featuring football icons, and tapping into the Gulf’s love of pickle‑forward flavors. With the tournament over and Spain crowned champions, the chips keep the football spirit alive on supermarket shelves. F&B brands entering the Gulf should note the winning formula: local produce, local sports stars, and a bold flavour profile.

GCC & SAUDI
Sabic mulls 50% stake in Chinese materials JV
Sabic signed a project development agreement with China’s Rongsheng Petrochemical for the Jintang New Materials Project in Zhejiang, and is evaluating an equity investment of up to 50%. The advanced materials venture would deepen Sabic’s footprint in China at a time when Gulf‑China industrial ties are tightening, but no investment figure has been disclosed.

The move comes after Sabic sold its European units in January for SAR 3.6 billion and returned to a slim net profit of SAR 13.2 million in the first quarter. With global growth slowing and oil prices volatile, petrochemical players are betting on high‑value specialties in Asia to preserve margins. The deal awaits final board approval. Investors should track how much of Sabic’s future capex shifts East as it exits Europe.
