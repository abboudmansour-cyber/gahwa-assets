The Lead
​
​
Two supertankers carrying 2.7 million barrels of Saudi crude did a U-turn in the Red Sea yesterday as Houthi rebels warned shippers to stay away from Saudi ports.
Two supertankers carrying 2.7 million barrels of Saudi crude reversed course in the Red Sea yesterday after Houthi rebels declared a maritime embargo on the Kingdom. The Xin Long Yang, laden with 2 million barrels for China, and the Rodos, with 700,000 barrels for India, turned away from the Yemeni coast, while a third tanker also aborted its approach off Oman. The Houthi warning explicitly told shipping companies not to load or discharge at any Saudi port.

War risk premiums on routes through Bab Al Mandeb more than doubled overnight, according to shipping sources. Brent crude shot up 9.3% to $96.32 on the day, reflecting the sudden repricing of supply-chain risk. The move puts roughly 7% of global seaborne oil trade in play and tests Saudi Arabia’s contingency plans.

This is no longer a rhetorical standoff. If shippers begin avoiding the Red Sea corridor en masse, Saudi Aramco faces a logistical headache: longer voyages around the Cape of Good Hope, higher freight costs, and the potential need to reshuffle Asian term cargoes. For Gulf economies still absorbing the cost of the US-Iran war, a simultaneous crisis on the Yemeni front tightens the fiscal and security screws. The US has warned the Houthis against blocking the waterway, but yesterday’s tanker U-turns show the market is already voting with its hulls.
Redirect tanker routing now, longer voyages and higher insurance will eat into spot margins for any Gulf-Asian crude trade.

MACRO & GEO
Kuwait intercepts Iranian drone attack
Kuwait’s military said it activated air defences to intercept hostile drones from Iran overnight, with residents reporting explosions in the sky. With Iran warning that no regional infrastructure will remain secure if its own security is not guaranteed, the risk of miscalculation grows each night. Kuwait’s oil infrastructure sits a short flight from Iran; any sustained campaign would threaten both production and investor confidence. Reassess physical security and continuity plans for assets in northern Gulf states, insurance and evacuation protocols need updating now.

FINTECH & CRYPTO
SILQ lands $20M for Saudi SME finance
SILQ, the B2B platform born from the merger of Sary and ShopUp, secured a $20 million Shariah-compliant structured financing facility from Gemcorp Capital to scale embedded finance for Saudi SMEs. The platform already serves more than 50,000 businesses through its procurement arm Sary and its lending engine Fina, enabling over SAR 10 billion in B2B commerce this year.

The money will flow into invoice financing and trade finance, directly addressing the working-capital squeeze that small Saudi firms complain about most. For Gemcorp, the deal marks its first direct lending, first Shariah-compliant facility, and first mezzanine transaction in the Kingdom, a triple debut that signals institutional appetite for Saudi non-bank finance is finally catching up to the Vision 2030 rhetoric. Founders in B2B procurement and fintech can benchmark this structure as a template for Shariah-compliant mezzanine facilities in the Gulf.

AI & TECH
MGX closes $40bn data centre deal
The deal is the cornerstone of the AI Infrastructure Partnership, a vehicle that also includes Microsoft and Nvidia, which aims to mobilize roughly $30 billion in equity and up to $100 billion including debt.

For MGX, the move turns an Emirati sovereign-backed AI fund into a transatlantic hyperscale landlord. The firm has already raised more than $50 billion from regional sovereign and global pension funds and is targeting north of $100 billion in assets. That makes it the Gulf’s most muscular bet on the physical layer of the AI economy, land, power, and cooling, rather than on the models themselves. Operators looking for data-centre capacity or AI infrastructure partners should map MGX’s portfolio now; its buying spree is reshaping supply chains.

CULTURE
Saudi launches 90-day Umrah visa
The Hajj and Umrah Ministry launched a multiple-entry Umrah visa valid for one year, allowing cumulative stays of up to 90 days. That turns a traditional short pilgrimage into a flexible, repeat-visit travel product, with obvious upside for airlines, hotels, and ground services in Makkah and Madinah.

The move dovetails with Riyadh’s push to hit 30 million Umrah pilgrims a year by 2030. For a Kingdom that once rationed religious tourism tightly, a 90-day window with multiple entries is the closest thing to a premium loyalty scheme Islam has ever seen. Tour operators should repackage Umrah trips around the 90-day window, longer stays mean higher per-guest spend on accommodation and transport.

GCC & SAUDI
US-Saudi nuclear pact signed
President Trump signed a 30-year civil nuclear cooperation agreement with Saudi Arabia, clearing the path for the Kingdom to build reactors with American technology and to enrich its own uranium. Energy Secretary Chris Wright and Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman inked the deal alongside a separate safeguards arrangement.

The pact, reportedly worth tens of billions of dollars, locks Riyadh into a US-led nuclear supply chain and removes a major diplomatic friction point. For proponents of Vision 2030, domestic nuclear power promises to free up more oil for export while greening the grid. Critics will watch whether enrichment rights set a precedent for other regional powers. Nuclear engineering, construction, and fuel-cycle services firms should map the Saudi supply chain; the first reactor tenders will accelerate now.
