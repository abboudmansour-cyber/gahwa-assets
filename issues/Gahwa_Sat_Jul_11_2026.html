What happened
The US just invited the UAE to the grown-ups' AI table, and Abu Dhabi didn't even need a permission slip.
Abu Dhabi no longer needs a US permission slip to buy the world's most advanced AI chips.

The US Commerce Department moved the UAE from its restricted D:3/D:4 lists into the elite A:5 group, the first Arab nation to earn that status. The reclassification, a direct result of the May 2025 US-UAE AI Cooperation framework, gives Emirati government entities and approved companies like G42, Core42, Amazon, and Microsoft license-free access to advanced computing items, including Nvidia GPUs and dual-use technologies.

The move slashes red tape, previously, some 50 export license applications were needed yearly. Now the UAE stands alongside the UK, India, and South Korea, as Washington bets on Abu Dhabi as a trusted AI ally in a region roiling from Iran tensions.

For the GCC, the upgrade is a force multiplier. UAE-based data centers can now scale without Washington's stopwatch, solidifying the country's push to become a global AI hub. Gulf rivals will be watching: Abu Dhabi's first-mover advantage on chips may widen the tech gap within the region, creating a new axis of AI clout that extends from the desert to the data hall.
US BIS published the new rule on Wednesday, and Emirati firms can now order chips without a license.

MACRO & GEO
US-Iran Strikes Paralyse Hormuz Again
US jets hit roughly 90 targets inside Iran and Tehran struck US bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar, officials said, plunging the Strait of Hormuz deeper into crisis.

Daily tanker transits through the waterway have fallen to about 40 vessels, a fraction of the pre-war 125-140. Shipping insurance and freight rates are soaring, while global oil supply remains 9.4 million barrels per day below pre-crisis levels. President Trump called the ceasefire 'over' even as some officials insisted technical talks continue.

For Gulf economies, the calculus is brutal: elevated security risk keeps LNG and petrochemical carriers tied up, hurts tourism in Dubai and Ras Al Khaimah, and forces governments to find costly workarounds via pipelines and rail. A return to surplus depends entirely on de-escalation, a prospect that looks dimmer by the day. The exchange marks the heaviest US strikes on Iranian soil in years, killing a fragile truce.

FINTECH & CRYPTO
HSBC Tokenises Deposits in the UAE
HSBC is now letting UAE corporate clients move money as tokenized deposits, a regulated alternative to stablecoins that stays fully inside the banking system.

The rollout, first reported Wednesday, allows firms to settle treasury and cross-border payments 24/7 on a blockchain-based ledger without the volatility risk of third-party stablecoins. Kyle Boag, HSBC's MENAT head of global payments, said the product is a direct claim on the bank and operates under the same regulatory framework as a traditional deposit.

It's a shot across the bow of the UAE's stablecoin pioneers, AE Coin, Zand Bank, Rakbank, and comes as corporate stablecoin volumes in the country have surged over 40% year-on-year. Central bank rules require 1:1 backing and audited whitepapers; tokenized deposits promise the same speed while harnessing the bank's own balance sheet, a formula that may quiet treasurers who find stablecoins too exotic. HSBC launched the service this week, directly after the CBUAE's stablecoin framework took effect.

GCC & SAUDI
e& Exits Vodafone in $6bn Sale
UAE telecom giant e& is selling its entire 16% stake in Vodafone for $5.95 billion to focus on its own growth, booking a net cash return of AED4.7 billion.

The sale, to a vehicle called Vega, crystallises e&'s four-year flirtation with the British operator and sharpens its domestic focus. e&'s revenue jumped 15% in Q1 2026 to AED19.4 billion, and the share price is up more than 7% year-to-date. The proceeds give it dry powder for M&A or network buildout at home and across the MENA region. The transaction was announced this week, freeing up capital for e&'s regional ambitions.

CULTURE
Saudi Roaster Crowned MENA's Best Coffee
A Saudi specialty roaster just took the top prize at the Global Coffee Awards, signaling the Kingdom's quiet rise as a regional coffee tastemaker.

Woods Specialty Roastery won Best Roaster in the MENA category, while Brew 92 and Oasis Coffee Trading Company also collected gold medals. The win underscores a domestic coffee boom, Saudi Arabia's café scene is flourishing as social norms loosen and tourism surges.

For the region, premium coffee is no longer just a Lebanese or Emirati game; Saudi home-grown talent is now roasting at world-class levels, and the awards serve as a branding tool to attract international coffee tourists. The award ceremony just concluded, putting Saudi roasters on the global specialty map.

GCC & SAUDI
Dubai Rents Dip, RAK Surges 10%
New lease prices in Dubai fell 6% year-on-year in the first half to an average of AED 60,000, while Ras Al Khaimah rents jumped 10% to AED 57 per square foot, according to Dubai Land Department data.

The divergence tells a story of two Gulf property markets. Dubai's rental cooling, with a 20% drop in new lease volumes, reflects an exodus of price-sensitive tenants and a cautious corporate reset as regional tensions bite. In Ras Al Khaimah, the $5.1 billion Wynn casino resort is luring residents and hospitality workers, tightening supply sharply.

For operators, the message is clear: affordable Dubai is making a comeback, but the action is shifting north. First-half data just landed, showing a stark north-south split in UAE residential demand.
